Discovery Questions
What we'd need to understand before building anything
The first two weeks are purely about understanding the current state ‚Äî no AI, no building, just learning. These are the questions that would shape everything that follows. Click each category to expand.
  • What practice management or EHR system do you use today ‚Äî Athenahealth, Jane App, Kareo, or something else?
  • How do claims get submitted ‚Äî directly through the EHR, via a clearinghouse, or a more manual process?
  • Do any of your current tools have AI features built in? Are any of them actively used?
  • Does your EHR or billing platform expose an API for data access?
  • Where does payment posting and reconciliation happen ‚Äî in the EHR, a spreadsheet, or elsewhere?
  • Walk me through what happens step-by-step after a patient visit ends ‚Äî who does what, in what order?
  • Who owns billing today ‚Äî is it a dedicated role or split across people?
  • How many claims do you process per week or month?
  • How do you track claim status and follow up on aging or unpaid claims?
  • What does denial management look like in practice ‚Äî who handles it, what's the process, how long does each one take?
  • Which step in the billing process takes the longest or feels most error-prone?
  • What's your current denial rate, and what are the top 3 denial reasons?
  • How often do you write off denied claims rather than appealing ‚Äî and what drives that decision?
  • Are there specific payers that cause disproportionate headaches?
  • If your billing team got an hour back per day, what would they actually do with it?
  • How much historical claims data do you have access to, and in what format ‚Äî EHR export, spreadsheet, something else?
  • Do you currently track time spent per claim or per denial anywhere?
  • What's your average days-to-payment across your major payers?
  • How is the team expected to grow over the next 12 months ‚Äî and does billing volume scale with it?
  • What does a fully-loaded cost per claim look like today ‚Äî staff time, write-offs, rework?

How the discovery ends
Build vs. buy vs. neither
The discovery closes with a recommendation across one of these three paths. The right answer depends entirely on what the data shows ‚Äî there's no predetermined outcome.
üî®
Build
Makes sense when
  • Pilot shows clear time savings (2+ hrs/week recovered)
  • No vendor fits Elektra's payer mix or women's health specifics
  • Volume is growing ‚Äî ROI compounds over time
  • You want control over the AI logic and prompt behavior
üõí
Buy
Makes sense when
  • ROI is clear and a vendor exists at the right price point
  • EHR integration would be costly or complex to build internally
  • Compliance requirements favor a certified, audited vendor
  • Engineering capacity is limited or expensive
‚è∏
Neither (yet)
Makes sense when
  • Volume is too low to justify the investment right now
  • Current manual process is fast and accurate enough
  • Implementation overhead exceeds expected year-one savings
  • Data quality is too poor to produce reliable AI outputs
A note on vendors: Most healthcare billing AI vendors are wrapping the same general-purpose models (Claude, GPT-4) in a branded UI and charging $30‚Äì100k/year for it. Running the discovery first means Elektra understands the ROI before committing to anything ‚Äî and if a vendor turns out to be the right call, it's from a position of knowing exactly what to look for and what's worth paying for.
